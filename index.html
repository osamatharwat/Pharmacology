Introduction

What Are Fluoroquinolones?

Scientific Overview

Fluoroquinolones are broad-spectrum bactericidal antibiotics that inhibit bacterial DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV. This blocks DNA replication, causes DNA fragmentation, and ultimately leads to bacterial cell death. They were widely used because of their excellent oral bioavailability, strong tissue penetration, and convenient dosing.

High
oral bioavailability comparable to IV therapy
Strong
tissue penetration in lungs, kidneys, and prostate
Broad
coverage against many Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria
Mechanism of Action

How Fluoroquinolones Work

🧬

Bacterial DNA

Replication begins with unwinding and copying of bacterial DNA.

⚙️

DNA Gyrase

Primary target in Gram-negative bacteria; relieves torsional stress during replication.

🔩

Topoisomerase IV

Primary target in Gram-positive bacteria; separates replicated DNA strands.

💥

DNA Fragmentation

Enzyme inhibition blocks replication and causes rapid loss of DNA integrity.

☠️

Bacterial Death

Fluoroquinolones produce concentration-dependent bactericidal activity.

Clinical Evolution

Past vs Current Indications

Past Use

First-Line / Broad Use
  • ✓Uncomplicated urinary tract infections
  • ✓Acute bacterial sinusitis
  • ✓Acute bronchitis
  • ✓Mild respiratory tract infections
  • ✓Traveler’s diarrhea and mild GI infections
  • ✓Frequently used even when safer alternatives were available
Historical broad-spectrum use was driven by convenience, potency, and strong tissue penetration.

Current Use

Restricted / Selected Cases
  • ⚠Complicated urinary tract infections and pyelonephritis
  • ⚠Hospital-acquired pneumonia
  • ⚠Severe bacterial infections and sepsis
  • ⚠Multidrug-resistant organism infections
  • ⚠When no safer alternatives are available
  • ⚠Not recommended as first-line therapy for mild infections
Safety concerns shifted fluoroquinolones from routine therapy to restricted use.
FDA Safety Warnings

Major Adverse Effects

Click any card to reveal more clinical detail.

🦵

Tendon Rupture

Musculoskeletal toxicity

Tendinitis and tendon rupture, especially the Achilles tendon. Risk is higher in older adults and with corticosteroid use.

🧠

Neuropathy

May be irreversible

Peripheral neuropathy can present with pain, tingling, burning, or weakness and may persist long after therapy ends.

⚡

CNS Effects

Dizziness, hallucinations

Central nervous system toxicity includes dizziness, insomnia, agitation, confusion, hallucinations, and in severe cases seizures.

🩸

Dysglycemia

Hypo-/hyperglycemia

Blood glucose disturbances are more clinically important in diabetic patients, especially those receiving insulin or sulfonylureas.

❤️

Aortic Risk

Aneurysm / dissection

Fluoroquinolones are associated with increased risk of aortic aneurysm and dissection, especially in vulnerable patients.

Risk Factors

Who Is Most at Risk?

👴

Age > 60 Years

Higher rates of tendon and systemic adverse effects.

🫘

Renal Impairment

Reduced clearance increases exposure and toxicity.

💊

Corticosteroids

Greatly increases the risk of tendon injury and rupture.

📈

High Dose

Adverse effects increase with prolonged therapy.

🔁

Organ Transplant

Immunosuppressive treatment compounds risk.

🍬

Diabetes Mellitus

Higher likelihood of clinically significant dysglycemia.

Risk–Benefit Balance

Clinical Decision Framework

The key principle is simple: use fluoroquinolones only when benefits clearly outweigh risks.

Potential Benefits

  • ✓Broad-spectrum bactericidal activity
  • ✓Excellent tissue penetration
  • ✓High oral bioavailability
  • ✓Convenient once/twice daily dosing

Serious Risks

  • ×Tendon rupture and neuropathy
  • ×CNS toxicity and seizures
  • ×Dysglycemia and QT prolongation
  • ×Aortic aneurysm/dissection
Use fluoroquinolones only when therapeutic benefits clearly outweigh potential risks.
